Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

At one point in my career, I had a colleague who made some patronising comments, and inferred that certain people were beneath him, or that he could use his position or rank to get more perks or benefits compared to other people. This is something that bothered me for a long time. The ethical decision that I had to make was whether to confront that person and those comments, or whether to ignore them. Essentially, I ended up giving that person the feedback directly. This links into an ethic that I hold of people treating others equally and fairly regardless of their position or rank.

Some personal limitations that I have are to do with how much time I want to commit to work. I would like to enjoy my work and be fulfilled by it, but not to let it dominate my life. Essentially this boils down to maintaining a work / life balance.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

I remember working on shared assignments at university with people who would not pull their weight. I found this confronting and difficult, especially as the grades were the same for everyone whether they were a full contributor or not contributing at all.

I remember my own personal strategy in these situations was a non-confrotational approach, where I would prefer to either a) work extra hard to compensate for the slacking person if I wanted a good grade, or b) stoop down to that other persons level of effrt (if I was not concerned as much about the grade.)

In hindsight, I should have probably confronted these issues directly by providing feedback. This may have been more personally challenging, but ultimately it may have led to a more positive outcome without me holding onto personal resentment. In a group assignment, there is no obligation for someone to work hard, rather it becomes a personal expectation of the other person. By vocalising and exploring my own expectations of others, it may help to resolve issues, or otherwise agreeing to disagree, rather than 'suffering in silence'.
